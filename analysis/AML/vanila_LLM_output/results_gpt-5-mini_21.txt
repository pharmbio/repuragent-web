Below is the list of 20 candidate repurposing drugs, with the rationale for each and pointers to what to look for in the literature. These are intended as leads to investigate further, not experimental instructions.

1) Auranofin (anti‑rheumatic)
- Mechanism: inhibits thioredoxin reductase → increases ROS and induces apoptosis.
- Rationale: reported cytotoxicity in AML cell lines and primary AML samples; often tested as a redox‑targeting agent and in combination with other agents.

2) Disulfiram (alcohol‑dependence drug; active with copper)
- Mechanism: forms complexes that inhibit proteasome and aldehyde dehydrogenase; promotes ROS.
- Rationale: several studies show disulfiram + copper kills AML cells and leukemia stem‑like cells.

3) Ciclopirox olamine (antifungal)
- Mechanism: iron chelation and inhibition of ribonucleotide reductase; affects cell cycle.
- Rationale: shown anti‑leukemic activity in vitro and in vivo in some studies.

4) Nelfinavir (HIV protease inhibitor)
- Mechanism: induces ER stress, inhibits AKT signaling and proteasome‑related pathways.
- Rationale: tested in AML models as an apoptosis inducer and chemosensitizer.

5) Mebendazole (anthelmintic)
- Mechanism: tubulin disruption and multiple off‑target effects.
- Rationale: preclinical anticancer activity reported in hematologic malignancies including AML models.

6) Nitazoxanide (antiparasitic)
- Mechanism: mitochondrial/energetic disruption and modulation of autophagy pathways.
- Rationale: emerging preclinical evidence for antiproliferative activity in some cancers, including blood cancers.

7) Metformin (antidiabetic)
- Mechanism: complex I inhibition, AMPK activation, metabolic stress.
- Rationale: many studies report antiproliferative effects, synergy with other agents in AML models.

8) Statins (e.g., simvastatin, pravastatin)
- Mechanism: HMG‑CoA reductase inhibition → affects prenylation and cell signaling.
- Rationale: statins can induce apoptosis and sensitize AML cells to chemotherapy; several preclinical and early clinical investigations.

9) Valproic acid (antiepileptic with HDAC inhibitory activity)
- Mechanism: histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibition and epigenetic modulation.
- Rationale: has been evaluated in AML clinical trials (often in combination with hypomethylating agents).

10) Hydroxychloroquine (antimalarial; autophagy inhibitor)
- Mechanism: lysosomal/autophagy inhibition.
- Rationale: used in preclinical/clinical combinations to block protective autophagy in AML.

11) Itraconazole (antifungal)
- Mechanism: hedgehog/mTOR/angiogenesis pathway effects and inhibition of some transporters.
- Rationale: repurposed in oncology with preclinical evidence of antiproliferative activity.

12) Ibrutinib (BTK inhibitor; approved for B‑cell malignancies)
- Mechanism: BTK and off‑target kinase inhibition.
- Rationale: some AML subsets show BTK activity; preclinical studies show growth inhibition or synergy in AML lines.

13) Ruxolitinib (JAK1/2 inhibitor)
- Mechanism: JAK–STAT pathway inhibition.
- Rationale: may be active in AML cases with JAK/STAT activation or as a partner in combinations.

14) Celecoxib (COX‑2 inhibitor)
- Mechanism: COX‑2 pathway and pro‑apoptotic effects independent of COX‑2.
- Rationale: preclinical studies show reduced proliferation and enhanced apoptosis in some AML models.

15) Omeprazole / Other PPIs (proton pump inhibitors)
- Mechanism: alter lysosomal pH and can affect drug sequestration and autophagy.
- Rationale: reported to sensitize cancer cells to chemotherapeutics and impair survival pathways.

16) Salinomycin (antibiotic ionophore — not approved for human systemic use in many countries)
- Mechanism: ion transport disruption, reported activity against cancer stem‑like cells.
- Rationale: strong preclinical activity in some leukemia models (toxicity profile limits clinical translation).

17) Disulfiram relatives / copper‑binding small molecules
- Rationale: same general class as disulfiram; look for agents that form cytotoxic copper complexes.

18) Tamoxifen / Raloxifene (SERMs)
- Mechanism: modulation of estrogen receptor–related growth pathways and additional off‑target effects.
- Rationale: some reports of antiproliferative activity in leukemia cell lines; more exploratory.

19) Parenteral retinoids other than ATRA (e.g., bexarotene)
- Mechanism: nuclear receptor modulation and differentiation induction in subsets of AML.
- Rationale: some AML subgroups or combinations may be sensitive to rexinoids/retinoids.

20) Protonophore/mitochondrial‑targeting drugs (e.g., older agents tested preclinically)
- Mechanism: mitochondrial depolarization and metabolic collapse.
- Rationale: AML cells, especially LSCs, can be dependent on mitochondrial metabolism; mitochondrial‑targeting repurposed drugs have preclinical activity.
