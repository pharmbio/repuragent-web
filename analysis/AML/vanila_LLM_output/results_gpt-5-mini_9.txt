=== AML Drug Repurposing Suggestions ===

Prompt:
Suggest top 20 existing drugs that could be repurposed for acute myeloid leukemia (AML) treatment 
and provide experimentally testable concentrations for an IC50 assay. 
The drug should inhibit the proliferation of AML cell lines, particularly MOLM13.


==================================================

Results:

Below are 20 existing drugs (approved or well-characterized investigational agents) that have been reported or are mechanistically plausible as candidates for repurposing against AML — with special attention to MOLM‑13 (an FLT3‑ITD AML cell line). For each I give a short rationale and an experimentally testable IC50 concentration range and a recommended dilution scheme you can use directly in cell‑viability assays.

Important general guidance (apply to all compounds)
- MOLM‑13 is FLT3‑ITD positive, so FLT3 inhibitors and BCL‑2 inhibitors often show high potency; include FLT3 inhibitors as positive comparators.
- Use a broad concentration range that spans at least 4–6 log10 to capture nM→µM potencies. Typical practical approach: 10‑point, 3‑fold serial dilution (covers ~4.3 logs) or 8–12 point, 2–3 fold dilutions. Below I give a suggested top concentration and range for each drug.
- Assay conditions: seed MOLM‑13 at 5,000–20,000 cells/well in 96‑well plates (optimize for linear growth); incubate 72 h (many AML cell IC50s measured at 48–72 h); readout: CellTiter‑Glo or similar metabolic/cell‑viability assay. Keep final DMSO ≤0.5% (preferably ≤0.2%).
- Run technical triplicates and at least two biological replicates. Include vehicle (DMSO) control and a positive control (e.g., cytarabine and a potent FLT3 inhibitor such as quizartinib/gilteritinib).
- Some agents (decitabine/azacitidine) require longer exposure to see epigenetic effects — you can still run standard 72 h viability assays but interpret cautiously.

List of 20 candidates (name — mechanism / rationale — suggested IC50 testing range and dilution)
1) Gilteritinib (ASP2215)
- Mechanism/rationale: FDA‑approved FLT3 inhibitor; active against FLT3‑ITD lines (MOLM‑13).
- Test range: 10 µM → 0.1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold serial dilution (10 µM, 3.33 µM, 1.11 µM … 0.1 nM).

2) Quizartinib (AC220)
- Mechanism/rationale: highly potent FLT3 inhibitor; known activity in FLT3‑ITD AML models.
- Test range: 1 µM → 0.1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold (1 µM to 0.1 nM).

3) Midostaurin (PKC412)
- Mechanism/rationale: multi‑kinase (FLT3) inhibitor approved in FLT3‑mutant AML.
- Test range: 10 µM → 1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold (10 µM to 1 nM).

4) Sorafenib
- Mechanism/rationale: multi‑kinase inhibitor with FLT3 activity; used off‑label in FLT3 AML.
- Test range: 10 µM → 0.1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold.

5) Ponatinib
- Mechanism/rationale: multi‑kinase TKI with activity against FLT3; has activity in FLT3‑ITD models.
- Test range: 1 µM → 0.1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold.

6) Venetoclax (ABT‑199)
- Mechanism/rationale: BCL‑2 inhibitor with strong single‑agent or combinatorial activity in AML; clinical activity in AML.
- Test range: 10 µM → 0.1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold.

7) Cytarabine (Ara‑C)
- Mechanism/rationale: standard-of‑care antimetabolite for AML — good positive control.
- Test range: 100 µM → 1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold (100 µM to 1 nM).

8) Decitabine
- Mechanism/rationale: hypomethylating agent used in AML; may reduce proliferation over longer exposure.
- Test range: 100 µM → 1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold. Note: consider longer exposure (5–7 days) for epigenetic effects.

9) Azacitidine
- Mechanism/rationale: hypomethylating agent used in AML/MDS.
- Test range: 100 µM → 1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold. Longer exposures advisable for epigenetic endpoints.

10) Bortezomib
- Mechanism/rationale: proteasome inhibitor — reported activity/synergy in AML cell lines.
- Test range: 1 µM → 0.1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold.

11) Alvocidib (flavopiridol)
- Mechanism/rationale: CDK inhibitor with activity in AML clinical research; can induce apoptosis in AML lines.
- Test range: 10 µM → 0.1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold.

12) Omacetaxine mepesuccinate (homoharringtonine)
- Mechanism/rationale: protein‑synthesis inhibitor used in hematologic malignancies; cytotoxic to AML lines.
- Test range: 10 µM → 0.1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold.

13) Trametinib
- Mechanism/rationale: MEK inhibitor — relevant if MOLM‑13 or patient samples have RAS pathway activation; some AML subsets sensitive.
- Test range: 1 µM → 0.1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold.

14) Everolimus (or temsirolimus)
- Mechanism/rationale: mTOR inhibitors can reduce AML proliferation; used in combinations.
- Test range: 10 µM → 1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold.

15) Dasatinib
- Mechanism/rationale: multi‑kinase inhibitor (SRC/ABL) with some activity in AML cell lines; potential activity in combination.
- Test range: 1 µM → 0.1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold.

16) Niclosamide
- Mechanism/rationale: antiparasitic compound repeatedly identified in repurposing screens for anti‑cancer activity (mitochondrial uncoupling / Wnt / STAT3 inhibition); reported AML activity in vitro.
- Test range: 10 µM → 0.1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold. Note: low solubility — ensure proper formulation.

17) Mebendazole
- Mechanism/rationale: antihelminth reported to have anti‑cancer effects (MT disruption); hits in repurposing screens for AML.
- Test range: 10 µM → 0.1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold.

18) Simvastatin (or other statins)
- Mechanism/rationale: statins show pro‑apoptotic/anti‑proliferative effects in AML in vitro; potential adjuvant.
- Test range: 50 µM → 10 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold (top 50 µM due to lower potency in many reports).

19) Itraconazole
- Mechanism/rationale: antifungal with Hh/VEGFR/mTOR modulation; found in repurposing screens with anticancer signals.
- Test range: 10 µM → 0.1 nM. Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold.

20) Disulfiram (± Cu2+)
- Mechanism/rationale: ALDH inhibitor/metal‑dependent proteotoxicity in cancer cells; activity often copper‑dependent (test with ±CuCl2).
- Test range: 10 µM → 0.1 nM (test both without and with low µM Cu2+). Suggested: 10‑point, 3‑fold. Note: cytotoxic mechanism is metal‑dependent; include metal control.

Practical concentration/format recommendation (single standardized protocol)
- Use 10‑point, 3‑fold serial dilution unless a narrower range is preferred.
- Suggested top concentrations (one standard layout): Kinase inhibitors & potent targeted agents: start at 10 µM (or 1 µM for very potent TKIs such as quizartinib). Cytotoxics/hypomethylators: start at 100 µM. Natural products/repurposing hits: start 10 µM–50 µM depending on solubility/published potency.
- Example universal 10‑point, 3‑fold series starting at 10 µM: 10, 3.33, 1.11, 0.37, 0.12, 0.041, 0.014, 0.0046, 0.0015, 0.0005 µM (500 pM).
- For cytarabine/decitabine: a 100 µM top gives 100, 33.3, 11.1, 3.7, 1.23, 0.41, 0.14, 0.046, 0.015, 0.005 µM.

Controls and combination notes
- Positive controls: cytarabine and a potent FLT3 inhibitor (e.g., quizartinib or gilteritinib) to validate assay reliability on MOLM‑13.
- Consider testing venetoclax in combination with FLT3 inhibitors (strong published synergy in FLT3‑ITD AML).
- For agents like disulfiram, niclosamide, mebendazole and statins, test solubility and vehicle carefully and include vehicle‑only wells.

Caveats
- In vitro IC50 does not equal clinical efficacy; pharmacokinetics, toxicity, and target engagement in patients matter.
- Some repurposed drugs act via aggregation or off‑target mechanisms in vitro; confirm mechanism with orthogonal assays (e.g., apoptosis markers, target phosphorylation, rescue experiments).
- For epigenetic drugs (decitabine/azacitidine) and other agents with delayed kinetics, consider longer assays (5–7 days) or colony assays for accurate activity estimation.
