Top 20 candidate existing drugs to consider for AML repurposing (rationale and evidence)
Notes: many AML cell lines are genetically heterogeneous; MOLM‑13 is FLT3‑ITD positive, so FLT3 inhibitors are especially relevant. Where possible I note general evidence (preclinical cell‑line, xenograft, or clinical data) and mechanism of action.

1. Gilteritinib (FLT3 inhibitor)
- Rationale: Approved for FLT3‑mutated AML; direct FLT3 inhibition is effective in FLT3‑ITD cell lines and patients.
- Evidence: Clinical approval and multiple preclinical studies; active against FLT3‑ITD models such as MOLM‑13 in literature.

2. Quizartinib (AC220) (FLT3 inhibitor)
- Rationale: Potent FLT3 inhibitor with strong activity against FLT3‑ITD in preclinical models.
- Evidence: Preclinical AML cell‑line and xenograft data; explored in clinical trials.

3. Midostaurin (PKC/FLT3 multi‑kinase inhibitor)
- Rationale: Approved in combination for FLT3‑mutated AML; multi‑kinase activity can be useful in heterogeneous disease.
- Evidence: Clinical efficacy in FLT3 AML and preclinical activity in FLT3‑ITD cell lines.

4. Sorafenib (multi‑kinase inhibitor)
- Rationale: Inhibits FLT3 among other kinases; used off‑label and studied in FLT3‑ITD AML.
- Evidence: Preclinical and clinical reports of activity in FLT3‑mutant AML.

5. Ponatinib (multi‑TKI)
- Rationale: Broad kinase inhibition including some FLT3 mutants; investigated for resistant FLT3 variants.
- Evidence: Preclinical activity in FLT3 mutant models.

6. Venetoclax (BCL‑2 inhibitor)
- Rationale: Approved in AML combinations; potent inducer of apoptosis in many AML contexts.
- Evidence: Extensive preclinical and clinical data showing activity in AML and in combination therapies.

7. Selinexor (XPO1/CRM1 inhibitor)
- Rationale: Nuclear export inhibition causes accumulation of tumor suppressors and pro‑apoptotic factors.
- Evidence: Preclinical AML activity and early‑phase clinical investigations.

8. Auranofin (gold compound, thioredoxin reductase inhibitor)
- Rationale: Induces oxidative stress and apoptosis; broad preclinical antileukemic activity.
- Evidence: Multiple studies showing activity across AML cell lines, including those with FLT3 alterations in some reports.

9. Niclosamide (anthelmintic)
- Rationale: Multi‑pathway effects (Wnt, STAT3, mitochondrial uncoupling) and a frequent repurposing candidate.
- Evidence: Preclinical AML cytotoxicity reported in cell line screens.

10. Disulfiram (with copper) (aldehyde dehydrogenase inhibitor/copper‑dependent cytotoxicity)
- Rationale: Generates reactive species and proteasome inhibition when combined with copper; reported anticancer effects.
- Evidence: Preclinical AML reports showing cytotoxicity in cell lines.

11. Ibrutinib (BTK inhibitor)
- Rationale: BTK is expressed in some AML subsets; ibrutinib can sensitize AML cells and has been tested in combinations.
- Evidence: Preclinical activity in AML models and early clinical studies; synergy reported with other agents.

12. Acalabrutinib (BTK inhibitor)
- Rationale: Second‑generation BTK inhibitor with similar rationale to ibrutinib; potential lower off‑target effects.
- Evidence: Preclinical AML studies and interest in combination strategies.

13. Vorinostat (SAHA, HDAC inhibitor)
- Rationale: Epigenetic modulation can restore apoptotic programs; HDAC inhibitors have preclinical AML activity.
- Evidence: Preclinical AML data and clinical trials investigating combinations.

14. Trametinib (MEK inhibitor)
- Rationale: Targets MAPK pathway (useful for RAS pathway‑mutated AML); potential in NRAS/KRAS mutant cases.
- Evidence: Preclinical activity in RAS‑driven leukemia models and combination studies.

15. Ruxolitinib (JAK1/2 inhibitor)
- Rationale: JAK/STAT pathway inhibition in AML subsets with pathway activation or cytokine signaling dependence.
- Evidence: Preclinical signals and clinical investigations, often in combination.

16. Ciclopirox olamine (antifungal, iron chelator)
- Rationale: Alters iron metabolism and impairs ribonucleotide reductase; repurposing candidate with anticancer activity.
- Evidence: Preclinical AML cytotoxicity reported.

17. Simvastatin (and other statins)
- Rationale: Interfere with prenylation and cholesterol metabolism; show pro‑apoptotic effects and synergize with other agents.
- Evidence: Preclinical AML studies and some clinical exploratory work.

18. Metformin (biguanide)
- Rationale: Alters cellular metabolism/AMPK activation; reported to limit leukemia cell proliferation in some contexts.
- Evidence: Preclinical AML studies; interest in metabolic targeting.

19. Thioridazine (antipsychotic)
- Rationale: Reported to preferentially target leukemia stem cells and impair self‑renewal.
- Evidence: Preclinical studies demonstrating eradication of AML stem‑like cells in vitro.

20. Bortezomib (proteasome inhibitor)
- Rationale: Proteasome inhibition can induce apoptosis in myeloma and some AML contexts; investigated clinically in AML combinations.
- Evidence: Clinical trials and preclinical AML studies, often evaluated in combination regimens.

How to prioritize these for MOLM‑13 (conceptual, non‑actionable)

- Look for single‑agent activity in published MOLM‑13 datasets or large drug‑screen databases.
- Consider combinations with approved AML agents (azacitidine/decitabine, cytarabine, venetoclax) because repurposed single agents often require combination strategies.
- Assess safety/tolerability and achievable plasma exposures in humans (pharmacokinetics) when prioritizing for clinical repurposing.

- DepMap (Broad Institute) — cell‑line sensitivity screens and mutation status.
- GDSC (Genomics of Drug Sensitivity in Cancer) and CTRP (Cancer Therapeutics Response Portal) — large drug‑sensitivity datasets.
- PRISM (Broad) and LINCS L1000 perturbation signatures — for large‑scale profiling and signatures of response.
- Clinical trial registries and drug prescribing labels — for human PK/PD and safety information that help judge clinically achievable exposures.

Non‑procedural experimental design considerations (high level)
- Look for published dose–response curves in MOLM‑13 (or closely related FLT3‑ITD lines) to estimate likely potency and therapeutic window.
- Use orthogonal readouts (viability, apoptosis markers, long‑term clonogenic assays) when possible to confirm cytotoxic vs cytostatic effects.
- When prioritizing for clinical repurposing, weigh known human PK (Cmax, half‑life) and safety profile: potent in vitro activity at concentrations not achievable or safe in humans reduces translational potential.
