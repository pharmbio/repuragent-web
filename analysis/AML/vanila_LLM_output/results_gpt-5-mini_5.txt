How to prioritize among these for MOLM‑13
- Start with FLT3‑directed agents (gilteritinib, quizartinib, midostaurin, sorafenib) because MOLM‑13 harbors FLT3‑ITD and is typically sensitive to FLT3 inhibition.
- Consider BH3‑mimetics (venetoclax, navitoclax) as single agents or in combination; venetoclax is widely active in AML contexts.
- For repurposing (non‑oncology) drugs, auranofin, tigecycline, disulfiram, statins and antimalarials (chloroquine) have published AML activity and are attractive for combinations or secondary screening.
- Agents targeting proteostasis, CDKs, XPO1, HSP90, or mTOR are logical mechanistic classes to test given the vulnerabilities of leukemic cells.

Non‑actionable guidance on designing a dose–response screen / IC50 plan (what to consult, not procedural steps)
- Literature search: for each candidate, consult primary preclinical AML studies and any MOLM‑13–specific reports to see reported potency class and experimental assay formats (viability assays, incubation durations, readouts). PubMed, clinicaltrials.gov, and drug package inserts / reviews are good sources.
- Potency class informs screening range: kinase inhibitors and targeted agents are often active in the low‑nanomolar range; antibiotics, metabolic drugs and autophagy inhibitors often show effects at micromolar concentrations in cell‑based assays. Use this qualitative information to choose a broad, multi‑point dose–response range in your pilot experiments, and then refine.
- Include appropriate controls (vehicle, a positive control known to be active in your cell model) and replicate measures. Use robust viability readouts and curve‑fitting (nonlinear regression) to estimate half‑maximal inhibitory concentrations from the full dose–response.
- Consider combination testing for agents known to synergize (e.g., venetoclax combinations, FLT3 inhibitors + other targeted agents), but consult the literature for combination schedules and rationales.
- Check cell‑line authentication and mycoplasma status, and confirm readout linearity and dynamic range for the viability assay you plan to use (these are standard best‑practice checks reported in the literature).
