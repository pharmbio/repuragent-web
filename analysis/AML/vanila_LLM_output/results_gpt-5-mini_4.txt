Top 20 repurposing candidates (short rationale and evidence pointers)

1. Gilteritinib (ASP2215)
- Mechanism: potent FLT3/AXL inhibitor.
- Why relevant: Approved for relapsed/refractory FLT3‑mutant AML; active against FLT3‑ITD cell lines such as MOLM‑13 in many preclinical studies.
- Evidence pointer: clinical and preclinical FLT3 literature; search terms: “gilteritinib MOLM13 FLT3”.

2. Quizartinib (AC220)
- Mechanism: selective FLT3 inhibitor (FLT3‑ITD‑potent).
- Why relevant: Strong activity against FLT3‑ITD AML cell lines; often used in preclinical MOLM‑13/ MV4‑11 models.
- Evidence pointer: “quizartinib FLT3‑ITD MOLM13”.

3. Midostaurin (PKC412)
- Mechanism: multi‑kinase inhibitor including FLT3.
- Why relevant: Approved in combination therapy for newly diagnosed FLT3‑mutant AML; active in preclinical FLT3 models.
- Evidence pointer: “midostaurin FLT3 MOLM13”.

4. Sorafenib
- Mechanism: multi‑kinase inhibitor with FLT3 activity.
- Why relevant: Used off‑label and in trials for FLT3‑mutant AML; preclinical activity in FLT3‑ITD lines.
- Evidence pointer: “sorafenib FLT3 MOLM13”.

5. Ponatinib
- Mechanism: multi‑target TKI (including FLT3 at higher potency).
- Why relevant: Shown to inhibit some FLT3 mutants and AML cell proliferation in preclinical reports.
- Evidence pointer: “ponatinib FLT3 AML”.

6. Venetoclax
- Mechanism: BCL‑2 inhibitor (pro‑apoptotic).
- Why relevant: Approved in AML (combination regimens); shows potent activity in many AML cell lines and primary samples; rational combination with other agents.
- Evidence pointer: “venetoclax AML MOLM13”.

7. Auranofin
- Mechanism: thioredoxin reductase inhibitor; increases ROS and induces apoptosis.
- Why relevant: Multiple studies have reported auranofin’s activity against AML cell lines and primary AML blasts; reported activity in some FLT3‑ITD models.
- Evidence pointer: “auranofin AML thioredoxin reductase”.

8. Disulfiram (often studied with Cu2+)
- Mechanism: copper‑dependent proteasome and aldehyde dehydrogenase interference; ROS induction.
- Why relevant: Preclinical reports of cytotoxicity in AML cells when combined with copper; being evaluated for cancer repurposing.
- Evidence pointer: “disulfiram AML copper”.

9. Niclosamide
- Mechanism: mitochondrial uncoupler and Wnt/STAT3/Notch pathway inhibitor.
- Why relevant: Anthelmintic with multiple anticancer effects; reported anti‑proliferative activity in AML models and in some screens.
- Evidence pointer: “niclosamide AML MOLM13”.

10. Clofazimine
- Mechanism: likely multiple effects including mitochondrial/ion channel perturbation and NF‑κB modulation.
- Why relevant: Identified in drug screens for AML cytotoxicity; reported activity in AML models.
- Evidence pointer: “clofazimine AML”.

11. Mebendazole
- Mechanism: microtubule/tubulin disruption.
- Why relevant: Anti‑helminthic shown to inhibit growth of various cancer cell lines including some leukemia models in preclinical studies.
- Evidence pointer: “mebendazole AML”.

12. Simvastatin (and other statins)
- Mechanism: HMG‑CoA reductase inhibition; effects on prenylation and cell signaling.
- Why relevant: Statins have shown pro‑apoptotic effects in AML cell lines and have been studied in combination with standard agents.
- Evidence pointer: “statin AML simvastatin”.

13. Metformin
- Mechanism: AMPK activation, mitochondrial complex I inhibition.
- Why relevant: Epidemiologic and preclinical studies suggest anti‑proliferative effects in hematologic malignancies and potential to sensitize AML cells to other therapies.
- Evidence pointer: “metformin AML”.

14. Hydroxychloroquine / Chloroquine
- Mechanism: lysosomal/autophagy inhibition.
- Why relevant: Used in combination strategies to inhibit autophagy and sensitize AML cells to other agents in preclinical work.
- Evidence pointer: “hydroxychloroquine AML autophagy”.

15. Dasatinib
- Mechanism: SRC/ABL family kinase inhibitor (broad‑spectrum TKI).
- Why relevant: Has activity in subsets of AML and can synergize with other inhibitors in vitro; sometimes active against AML blasts dependent on SRC pathways.
- Evidence pointer: “dasatinib AML”.

16. Ruxolitinib
- Mechanism: JAK1/2 inhibitor.
- Why relevant: JAK‑STAT pathway inhibition can be relevant in AMLs with pathway activation; preclinical testing reports show variable AML cell line responses.
- Evidence pointer: “ruxolitinib AML”.

17. Trametinib (and other MEK inhibitors)
- Mechanism: MEK1/2 inhibitor.
- Why relevant: AML cases with RAS pathway activation can be sensitive to MEK inhibition; tested in AML cell lines and xenografts.
- Evidence pointer: “trametinib AML RAS”.

18. Itraconazole
- Mechanism: antifungal with off‑target effects on hedgehog, angiogenesis and mTOR pathways.
- Why relevant: Identified in repurposing screens for anticancer activity; some preclinical data in hematologic malignancies.
- Evidence pointer: “itraconazole AML”.

19. Celecoxib
- Mechanism: COX‑2 inhibitor (also has COX‑independent effects).
- Why relevant: Reported to have pro‑apoptotic effects in AML cell lines and has been examined in combination with chemotherapy in preclinical studies.
- Evidence pointer: “celecoxib AML”.

20. Thioridazine (or other phenothiazines)
- Mechanism: antipsychotic with multiple off‑target effects (dopamine receptor, mitochondrial effects).
- Why relevant: Phenothiazines emerged in screens for cancer stem cell and leukemia cytotoxicity; reported activity in some AML models.
- Evidence pointer: “thioridazine AML cancer stem cell”.

Notes and caveats
- FLT3‑targeting TKIs (entries 1–5) are the most directly relevant to FLT3‑ITD lines such as MOLM‑13, because MOLM‑13 carries FLT3‑ITD and is often used to test FLT3 inhibitors.
- Some drugs listed (e.g., gilteritinib, midostaurin, venetoclax) are already approved for AML in specific settings; others are non‑oncology drugs identified by screens and need thorough preclinical validation.
- Single‑agent in vitro activity does not guarantee in vivo efficacy or clinical utility. Consider mechanism, achievable clinical exposure, toxicity profile, and combination strategies when prioritizing.
- For many of these agents, the strongest evidence is from screens, cell‑line studies, combination studies, or small preclinical reports—confirmatory experiments in multiple AML models and primary patient cells are recommended.
