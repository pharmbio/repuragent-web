1) Gilteritinib (FLT3 inhibitor)
- Rationale: Potent, clinically approved FLT3 inhibitor for FLT3‑mutated relapsed/refractory AML.
- Evidence: Active vs. FLT3‑ITD cell lines (including MOLM13) and patient samples; approved therapy for FLT3‑mutated AML.

2) Quizartinib (AC220; FLT3 inhibitor)
- Rationale: Very potent FLT3 inhibitor with strong activity in FLT3‑ITD models.
- Evidence: Demonstrated low‑nanomolar activity in FLT3‑ITD AML cell lines (MOLM13 among them in many studies).

3) Midostaurin
- Rationale: Multi‑kinase/FLT3 inhibitor approved in combination with chemotherapy for FLT3‑mutated AML.
- Evidence: Activity in FLT3‑mutant models; used clinically.

4) Sorafenib
- Rationale: Multi‑kinase inhibitor with FLT3 activity; used off‑label and in clinical studies in AML.
- Evidence: Inhibits growth of FLT3‑ITD cell lines; some combination activity reported with hypomethylating agents.

5) Venetoclax (BCL‑2 inhibitor)
- Rationale: Approved in combination regimens for AML; targets apoptosis regulation.
- Evidence: Demonstrated activity in many AML cell lines and primary blasts; often tested in MOLM13 and related lines especially in combination therapies.

6) Bortezomib (proteasome inhibitor)
- Rationale: Alters protein homeostasis and NF‑κB signaling; tested in AML.
- Evidence: In vitro activity in AML models and studied in clinical trials as combination therapy.

7) Omacetaxine mepesuccinate (homoharringtonine)
- Rationale: Protein synthesis inhibitor with activity in hematologic malignancies.
- Evidence: FDA‑approved for CML; reported activity in AML cell lines and some clinical experience.

8) Auranofin
- Rationale: Thioredoxin reductase inhibitor that induces ROS and proteotoxic stress; repositioning candidate for several cancers.
- Evidence: Reported cytotoxicity in AML cell lines, including some FLT3‑ITD lines in preclinical studies.

9) Arsenic trioxide
- Rationale: Highly effective in APL and can affect other leukemia subtypes via apoptosis and differentiation pathways.
- Evidence: Strong APL data; some activity reported in other AML preclinical models.

10) Disulfiram (often with copper)
- Rationale: Inhibits proteasome and induces oxidative stress in cancer cells; repurposing candidate.
- Evidence: Reported antiproliferative effects in leukemia models when combined with copper; some in vitro AML data.

11) Clofazimine
- Rationale: Anti‑mycobacterial drug with reported anticancer effects through NF‑κB/mitochondrial pathways.
- Evidence: In vitro activity reported in hematologic cancer models.

12) Niclosamide
- Rationale: Antihelminthic with multiple anticancer mechanisms (Wnt, STAT3, mTOR, mitochondrial uncoupling).
- Evidence: Anti‑leukemic activity reported in vitro (varies by cell line).

13) Mebendazole
- Rationale: Antihelminthic that binds tubulin and has shown anticancer activity in preclinical studies.
- Evidence: Some studies report antileukemic effects in vitro and in vivo models.

14) Metformin
- Rationale: Affects mitochondrial metabolism and AMPK signaling; investigated as adjuvant anticancer agent.
- Evidence: Sensitizes AML cells to other agents in some preclinical studies.

15) Statins (e.g., simvastatin)
- Rationale: Inhibit cholesterol biosynthesis and prenylation; show cytotoxic or sensitizing effects in AML in vitro.
- Evidence: Preclinical studies and some clinical correlative data suggest additive/chemosensitizing effects.

16) Ruxolitinib (JAK1/2 inhibitor)
- Rationale: Targets JAK‑STAT signaling which is relevant in subsets of AML or as combination therapy.
- Evidence: Activity in JAK‑ mutated contexts; explored in combinations for secondary AML/MF‑blast transformation.

17) Dasatinib
- Rationale: Multi‑kinase inhibitor (SRC/ABL) that can have off‑target anti‑AML effects and can synergize with other agents.
- Evidence: Variable single‑agent activity in AML; sometimes active in combinations.

18) Ibrutinib
- Rationale: BTK inhibitor with effects on non‑canonical signaling; explored in myeloid malignancies.
- Evidence: Limited single‑agent activity in AML, but some preclinical reports of synergy.

19) Trametinib (MEK inhibitor)
- Rationale: Inhibits MAPK pathway; may be active in AML subgroups with MAPK pathway activation or in combination approaches.
- Evidence: Preclinical activity reported in AML models, often in combination.

20) Celecoxib (COX‑2 inhibitor)
- Rationale: Anti‑inflammatory drug with antiproliferative and pro‑apoptotic effects reported in cancer models.
- Evidence: Some reports of antiproliferative activity in leukemia cell lines; of interest mainly as an adjunct.
