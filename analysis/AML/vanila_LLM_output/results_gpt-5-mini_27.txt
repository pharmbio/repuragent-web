Top 20 repurposing candidates (short rationale + where to read more)
1. Sorafenib (Nexavar) — multi‑kinase inhibitor with FLT3 activity; shown activity in FLT3‑ITD AML models (including MOLM13/related lines). See clinical and preclinical FLT3 literature and cohort studies of sorafenib in FLT3‑mutant AML.
2. Midostaurin (Rydapt) — multi‑kinase/FLT3 inhibitor approved for FLT3‑mutant AML; effective in FLT3‑driven cell models and clinical settings.
3. Gilteritinib (Xospata) — selective FLT3 inhibitor approved for relapsed/refractory FLT3 AML; strong preclinical activity in FLT3‑ITD cell lines.
4. Quizartinib (AC220) — potent FLT3 inhibitor with preclinical activity in FLT3‑ITD models; useful for comparing on‑target activity in MOLM13.
5. Venetoclax (Venclexta) — BCL‑2 inhibitor now used clinically in AML combinations; widely active across AML cell lines and used in MOLM13 combination studies.
6. Auranofin — gold compound (anti‑rheumatic) that induces oxidative/proteotoxic stress and apoptosis in AML cells in vitro; reported AML cytotoxicity in preclinical studies.
7. Disulfiram (Antabuse; usually with copper) — anti‑alcoholism drug; copper‑dependent proteotoxic and proteasome inhibition effects reported to kill various cancer cells, including AML models.
8. Niclosamide — anthelmintic repositioning candidate; reported inhibition of STAT3/Wnt and anti‑proliferative effects in AML preclinical studies.
9. Metformin — anti‑diabetic with reported anti‑proliferative effects and chemo‑sensitization in AML models (often investigated in combinations).
10. Hydroxychloroquine / chloroquine — autophagy inhibitors investigated in AML combinations to overcome cytoprotective autophagy.
11. Statins (e.g., simvastatin/lovastatin) — HMG‑CoA reductase inhibitors reported to induce apoptosis and synergy with standard agents in AML models.
12. Bortezomib (Velcade) — proteasome inhibitor used in myeloma; has shown activity and synergy in AML preclinical models and clinical trials in combinations.
13. Ibrutinib (Imbruvica) — BTK inhibitor with reported activity in subsets of AML and in leukemic stem cell contexts; investigated for off‑target effects in AML.
14. Ruxolitinib (Jakafi) — JAK1/2 inhibitor; explored in AML and post‑MPN AML settings and as combination therapy.
15. Arsenic trioxide (Trisenox) — established for APL; has mechanistic activity (oxidative/degradation pathways) that has been explored in non‑APL AML models.
16. Plerixafor (AMD3100) — CXCR4 antagonist used clinically for stem‑cell mobilization; shown to disrupt leukemia‑niche interactions and sensitize AML blasts to therapy.
17. Tranylcypromine (and derivatives) — MAO inhibitor that also inhibits LSD1 (KDM1A) at pharmacologically relevant levels; LSD1 inhibition is a differentiation strategy in AML under clinical investigation.
18. Dasatinib (Sprycel) — multi‑kinase (SRC/ABL) inhibitor with activity in AML subtypes and shown to have effects in some AML cell lines.
19. Lenalidomide (Revlimid) — immunomodulatory drug with activity in MDS/overlap AML and explored for combinations in AML trials.
20. Nicotinamide / NAD metabolism modulators (e.g., agents that modulate NAMPT pathways) — metabolic vulnerabilities in AML; several clinically used or clinically‑tested agents target these pathways and show preclinical AML activity.

Why these were picked
- Several are direct FLT3 inhibitors or are active in FLT3‑ITD models (sorafenib, midostaurin, gilteritinib, quizartinib) — MOLM13 is FLT3‑ITD, so those are natural positive controls and therapeutic candidates.
- Others target apoptosis regulators (venetoclax), proteostasis/oxidative stress (auranofin, bortezomib, disulfiram+copper), autophagy (hydroxychloroquine), metabolic vulnerabilities (metformin, statins, NAD pathways), epigenetic/differentiation regulators (tranylcypromine/LSD1 targets), or microenvironment signaling (plerixafor).
- Many of these drugs are already clinically approved for other indications, have established safety profiles, and/or appear in publicly available drug‑sensitivity datasets for cancer cell lines.

Where to find reported potency and MOLM13‑specific data
- Public pharmacogenomic/drug‑sensitivity resources: DepMap / PRISM, GDSC (Genomics of Drug Sensitivity in Cancer), CTRP (Cancer Therapeutics Response Portal), ChEMBL and PubChem BioAssays. These resources often include measured IC50/AUC values for many drugs across common cancer cell lines (including MOLM13 in many datasets).
- PubMed searches with terms like “MOLM13 + [drug name] + IC50” or “[drug name] + AML cell line + MOLM13” will retrieve primary studies that report dose–response results and experimental conditions.
- Reviews and clinical translational papers summarizing preclinical efficacy in FLT3‑ITD or MOLM13 models (search for FLT3‑ITD reviews, or “MOLM13 model AML”).

High‑level, non‑operational guidance for planning an IC50/viability study (conceptual only)
- Use the literature and public datasets above to get an expected potency order of magnitude per compound before ordering materials.
- Assay design should include appropriate negative (vehicle) and positive controls, multiple biological replicates, and at least one later timepoint (cells/treatment duration chosen per standard institutional protocols).
- Consider both single‑agent testing and combinations (especially for agents known to synergize, e.g., venetoclax combinations).
- Record and report units (molar units are standard), solvent system, and any co‑treated agents (e.g., copper with disulfiram) when applicable.
- Work with experienced cell‑biology colleagues and institutional biosafety/ethics committees to ensure appropriate approvals and safe handling.
