Top 20 repurposing candidates for AML (notes on mechanism / why they are of interest)
- Venetoclax — selective BCL‑2 inhibitor; established single‑agent/combination activity in AML and widely tested in AML cell lines and primary samples. (Clinical use in AML combinations.)
- Auranofin — gold compound that inhibits thioredoxin reductase and increases ROS; several studies report AML cytotoxicity and synergy with other agents.
- Tigecycline — antibiotic that inhibits mitochondrial translation; reported to selectively impair AML stem/progenitor cells by targeting mitochondrial function.
- Niclosamide — anthelmintic with pleiotropic effects (Wnt/β‑catenin, NF‑κB, mTOR); identified in screens as cytotoxic to several AML lines.
- Disulfiram (often tested with copper) — proposed to induce proteotoxic/oxidative stress and inhibit ALDH; reported AML activity in some studies.
- Nelfinavir — HIV protease inhibitor that induces ER stress/UPR and apoptosis; shows anti‑leukemia activity in preclinical work.
- Bortezomib — proteasome inhibitor used in myeloma; has been evaluated in AML preclinical and some clinical studies, sometimes in combination regimens.
- Ibrutinib — BTK inhibitor; BTK signaling is implicated in subsets of AML and preclinical activity has been reported.
- Dasatinib — multi‑kinase inhibitor (SRC/ABL); has activity against some AML signaling pathways and has been tested in AML models.
- Ruxolitinib — JAK1/2 inhibitor; of interest in AML cases with JAK/STAT pathway activation and for combination approaches.
- Crizotinib — ALK/MET inhibitor; relevant when targetable rearrangements or dependencies occur in AML.
- Metformin — biguanide that alters cellular metabolism and AMPK signaling; has been reported to sensitize AML cells to other drugs and reduce proliferation.
- Atorvastatin / Simvastatin — HMG‑CoA reductase inhibitors; statins have been reported to affect AML cell survival and to show synergy with other agents.
- Pimozide — neuroleptic reported to inhibit STAT5 and to have anti‑leukemic activity in some preclinical models.
- Quinacrine — antimalarial/antiprotozoal that can intercalate DNA and modulate p53; identified in some AML screens.
- Doxycycline — tetracycline antibiotic that impairs mitochondrial translation; related mitochondrial‑targeting effects have been reported in AML.
- Sertraline — SSRI reported in some drug‑repurposing screens to reduce proliferation in cancer cell lines including hematologic lines (mechanism not fully defined).
- Digoxin / cardiac glycosides — inhibit Na+/K+ ATPase; cardiac glycosides have shown anti‑proliferative effects in some leukemia assays.
- Fluspirilene (and other antipsychotics) — CNS drugs that have emerged in phenotypic screens with cytotoxic activity in AML lines.
- Discrete targeted kinase inhibitors used outside AML (e.g., crizotinib, dasatinib) — depending on a sample’s molecular profile, these are sometimes active.

How to check whether these agents inhibit MOLM13 specifically
- Public pharmacogenomic screens and drug sensitivity resources: DepMap/PRISM, CTRP (Cancer Therapeutics Response Portal), GDSC, and CCLE often include MOLM13 and report measured sensitivities (AUC/IC50) for many drugs. Searching these resources for “MOLM13” and the drug name will show whether there are published activity measurements.
- Literature searches: PubMed searches for the drug name + “AML” or “MOLM13” will retrieve studies that tested the compound in AML cell lines. Review articles and drug‑repurposing screens in AML will often list the most promising hits and the AML lines tested.

High‑level guidance for selecting concentration ranges for an IC50 assay (conceptual only)
- Teams typically consult published potency measurements (IC50, EC50, AUC) from public datasets and primary literature and then choose a concentration range that spans several orders of magnitude around reported potency.
- A standard approach is to test a series of spaced concentrations that allow construction of a full dose–response curve (coverage above and below expected potency), and ensure that readout windows include maximal effect and no‑effect baselines.
- Analyze dose–response data with nonlinear curve fitting to estimate IC50 (or other metrics like AUC), and include appropriate vehicle and positive/negative controls.
